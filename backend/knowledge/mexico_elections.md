# Mexico Election Process

## Constitutional Framework and System of Government

Mexico is a federal presidential constitutional republic governed by the 1917 Constitution. The system features separation of powers among the executive (President), legislative (Congress of the Union), and judicial branches. The President serves as head of state and government for a single six-year term (sexenio) with no re-election. The Congress is bicameral: the Chamber of Deputies (500 seats: 300 majority + 200 proportional) and the Senate (128 seats: 64 majority + 32 first minority + 32 proportional). Mexico has 32 federal entities (31 states and Mexico City).

## Election Administration Body

The National Electoral Institute (Instituto Nacional Electoral/INE) is the autonomous constitutional body that organizes federal elections. INE also coordinates with 32 state-level Public Local Electoral Bodies (OPLEs) for local elections. The Electoral Tribunal of the Federal Judiciary (TEPJF) resolves electoral disputes. INE manages the voter registry, credentials, and election logistics. Website: www.ine.mx

## Types of Elections

- **Presidential election**: Direct popular vote, single-round plurality
- **Federal legislative elections**: Chamber of Deputies (every 3 years) and Senate (every 6 years)
- **State elections**: Governors (6-year terms), state congresses, municipal presidents
- **Federal elections coincide**: President, Senate, and Chamber of Deputies every 6 years; midterm Chamber of Deputies election at 3 years
- Consecutive re-election: Deputies up to 4 terms, Senators up to 2 terms (since 2018 reform)

## Voter Registration Process

All Mexican citizens aged 18 and above are eligible and required to vote (though non-voting carries no practical penalty). Voters must obtain a Voter Credential (Credencial para Votar/INE card) with photograph, which also serves as the primary national ID. Registration is done at INE modules throughout the country. The credential includes biometric data and security features. The voter registry (Padrón Electoral) and electoral roll (Lista Nominal) are continuously updated.

## Election Timeline/Calendar

- **Pre-campaign period (intercampaña)**: January–March of election year
- **Campaign period**: 90 days for presidential race, 60 days for midterms
- **Campaign silence (veda)**: 3 days before election
- **Election day (Jornada Electoral)**: First Sunday of June (since 2024)
- **Results declaration**: Within days to weeks
- **Inauguration**: October 1 (President, since 2024 reform; previously December 1)

## Voting Methods and Technology

Mexico uses paper ballots printed by INE with security features (watermarks, microprinting, foil). Each ballot is unique to the electoral district and election type. Voters mark ballots with a crayon-like marker in voting booths at polling stations (casillas). Casillas are operated by randomly selected citizens (funcionarios de casilla) who receive training. There is no electronic voting at the federal level. Special polling stations allow voters outside their district to cast ballots.

## Campaign Rules and Finance

Public financing for parties is calculated annually based on a constitutional formula. Private donations are limited and must be from Mexican citizens or companies. Foreign funding is prohibited. INE allocates free airtime on radio and television (no paid political advertising is permitted). Campaign spending limits are set by INE for each election. The Fiscalización (audit) unit of INE monitors party finances in real time. Violations can result in fines or election annulment.

## Counting and Results Process

Votes are counted at each casilla by citizen poll workers (funcionarios) in the presence of party representatives. Results are recorded on tally sheets (actas) posted publicly outside the casilla. A Preliminary Electoral Results Program (PREP) provides quick unofficial results on election night. District Councils conduct the official count (cómputo distrital) within one week. Results are certified and can be challenged before the TEPJF.

## Post-Election Procedures

The TEPJF resolves all election disputes and validates the presidential election. The Electoral Tribunal's decisions are final and binding. Elected officials are proclaimed after dispute resolution. The presidential transition period runs from election day to inauguration. INE publishes comprehensive election reports and statistics. Post-election audits of campaign finances are conducted.

## Key Legislation

- Political Constitution of the United Mexican States (1917, as amended)
- General Law of Electoral Institutions and Procedures (LGIPE)
- General Law of Political Parties
- General Law on Electoral Crimes
- Law of Means of Challenge in Electoral Matters (LGSMIME)
- INE Regulations and Agreements
