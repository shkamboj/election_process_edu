# Japan Election Process

## Constitutional Framework and System of Government

Japan is a unitary parliamentary constitutional monarchy governed by the 1947 Constitution. The Emperor is the ceremonial head of state with no political power. The Prime Minister is the head of government, elected by the National Diet. The Diet is bicameral: the House of Representatives (Shūgiin, 465 seats) is the lower house, and the House of Councillors (Sangiin, 248 seats) is the upper house. The Prime Minister must maintain the confidence of the House of Representatives.

## Election Administration Body

Elections are administered by the Ministry of Internal Affairs and Communications at the national level and by Election Administration Commissions at prefectural and municipal levels. The Central Election Management Council provides guidance on election laws. Each of Japan's 47 prefectures has its own Election Administration Commission. Website: www.soumu.go.jp

## Types of Elections

- **House of Representatives elections**: 289 single-member districts (SMD) + 176 proportional representation (PR) in 11 regional blocks (parallel system)
- **House of Councillors elections**: 148 prefectural districts + 100 national proportional representation; half elected every three years
- **Local elections**: Prefectural governors, prefectural assemblies, mayors, municipal assemblies
- **Unified local elections**: Held every four years in April
- House of Representatives: maximum four-year term (often dissolved earlier)

## Voter Registration Process

All Japanese citizens aged 18 and above (lowered from 20 in 2016) are eligible to vote. Voter registration is automatic based on the Basic Resident Register (jūmin kihon daichō). Citizens registered as residents in a municipality for at least three months are automatically added to the voter list. Voter lists are prepared by municipal Election Administration Commissions. No separate registration action is needed by voters. Overseas Japanese can register for absentee voting at embassies.

## Election Timeline/Calendar

- **House of Representatives**: Dissolution triggers election within 40 days
- **House of Councillors**: Regular election every three years (typically in July)
- **Campaign period**: 12 days for House of Representatives, 17 days for House of Councillors
- **Unified local elections**: April every four years
- **Advance voting**: Available during the campaign period (typically 6-7 days before election)

## Voting Methods and Technology

Japan uses a distinctive system where voters write the name of their preferred candidate (or party for PR) by hand on a blank ballot (jisho-shiki). This contrasts with most countries that use pre-printed ballots. Ballots are made of synthetic paper that unfolds automatically for easier counting. Vote counting is conducted at designated counting centers using both manual counting and automatic sorting machines. Electronic voting has been trialed in some local elections but is not widely adopted.

## Campaign Rules and Finance

Japan's Public Offices Election Act strictly regulates campaigning. Door-to-door canvassing is prohibited. Candidates receive equal access to public resources: official campaign posters on designated boards, free NHK broadcast time, and government-delivered policy leaflets. Campaign vehicles with loudspeakers are permitted during designated hours. Individual donations are limited. Corporate donations to individual candidates are banned (allowed to parties). Political funds reports must be publicly disclosed annually.

## Counting and Results Process

After polls close (typically 8 PM), ballot boxes are transported to counting centers. Votes are sorted and counted publicly, often with citizen observers. Automatic sorting machines assist in reading handwritten ballots. Results are reported from counting centers to Election Administration Commissions. Preliminary results are available on election night through NHK and media reporting. Official results are certified within days.

## Post-Election Procedures

The new Diet convenes within 30 days of a House of Representatives election. The Prime Minister is elected by both houses (House of Representatives prevails in case of disagreement). Election disputes can be filed with High Courts within 30 days. The Supreme Court is the final arbiter. Election violation cases are handled by criminal courts. The Ministry of Internal Affairs publishes official election statistics.

## Key Legislation

- Constitution of Japan (1947), particularly Articles 15, 43-47, 93, 96
- Public Offices Election Act (1950, frequently amended)
- Political Funds Control Act (1948)
- Political Parties Subsidy Act (1994)
- Act on Special Provisions for the House of Representatives Electoral Districts
- Local Autonomy Act
