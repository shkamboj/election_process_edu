# United States Election Process

## Constitutional Framework and System of Government

The United States is a federal constitutional republic with a presidential system. The Constitution of 1787, along with its 27 amendments, establishes a separation of powers among three branches: executive (President), legislative (Congress), and judicial (Supreme Court). The federal system divides authority between the national government and 50 state governments. The President serves as both head of state and head of government for a four-year term, limited to two terms by the 22nd Amendment.

## Election Administration Body

Elections are administered at the state and local level, with over 8,000 election jurisdictions. The Federal Election Commission (FEC) oversees campaign finance law. The Election Assistance Commission (EAC) provides guidance and resources. Each state has a Secretary of State or equivalent official responsible for election administration. Website: www.fec.gov, www.eac.gov

## Types of Elections

- **Presidential elections**: Held every four years using the Electoral College system (538 electors)
- **Congressional elections**: All 435 House seats every two years; 33-34 of 100 Senate seats every two years
- **Midterm elections**: Congressional elections in non-presidential years
- **State and local elections**: Governors, state legislatures, mayors, school boards
- **Primary elections**: Party nomination contests (open, closed, or semi-closed depending on state)

## Voter Registration Process

Citizens aged 18 and older are eligible to vote. Registration is handled at the state level, with most states requiring advance registration (deadlines vary from 30 days to same-day). The National Voter Registration Act (1993) allows registration at DMV offices and public agencies. Some states offer automatic voter registration. Online registration is available in most states.

## Election Timeline/Calendar

- **Primary season**: January–June of election year
- **National conventions**: July–August
- **General election**: First Tuesday after the first Monday in November
- **Electoral College vote**: First Monday after the second Wednesday in December
- **Inauguration**: January 20 (President), January 3 (Congress)

## Voting Methods and Technology

Methods vary by state and include optical scan paper ballots, direct-recording electronic (DRE) machines, ballot-marking devices, and hand-marked paper ballots. Early voting is available in most states (ranging from 4 to 46 days before Election Day). Mail-in/absentee voting is available in all states, with some states conducting all-mail elections. Provisional ballots are available for voters whose eligibility is in question.

## Campaign Rules and Finance

Campaign finance is regulated by the FEC under the Federal Election Campaign Act. Individual contribution limits apply ($3,300 per candidate per election as of 2024). Super PACs may raise unlimited funds but cannot coordinate with campaigns (Citizens United v. FEC, 2010). Disclosure requirements mandate reporting of contributions over $200. Public financing is available for presidential campaigns through the Presidential Election Campaign Fund.

## Counting and Results Process

Votes are counted at the precinct level and aggregated by counties, then states. Media organizations project winners based on exit polls and partial results. Official canvassing takes days to weeks. States certify results according to their own deadlines. The Electoral College formally elects the President. Recounts are triggered automatically or by request depending on state law.

## Post-Election Procedures

Transition of power occurs between Election Day and Inauguration Day. The Presidential Transition Act facilitates orderly transfer. Election results can be challenged through state courts and, in limited cases, federal courts. Congress certifies Electoral College results on January 6. Audits and post-election reviews are conducted per state law.

## Key Legislation

- Constitution of the United States (1787) and Amendments (especially 15th, 19th, 24th, 26th)
- Voting Rights Act of 1965
- National Voter Registration Act of 1993 (Motor Voter Act)
- Help America Vote Act of 2002
- Federal Election Campaign Act of 1971
- Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act of 2002 (McCain-Feingold)
