# Electoral System in India

## First-Past-The-Post (FPTP) System
India uses the FPTP system for Lok Sabha and Vidhan Sabha elections:
- Each constituency elects **one representative**
- The candidate who receives the **most votes wins** (simple plurality, not majority)
- A candidate can win even with less than 50% of the total votes
- This system tends to favor larger parties and can lead to a party winning a large majority of seats without a majority of votes

### Example
If Candidate A gets 35%, B gets 30%, C gets 25%, D gets 10% — Candidate A wins, even though 65% of voters voted against them.

## Constituencies and Delimitation
- India is divided into **543 Lok Sabha constituencies** and varying numbers of state assembly constituencies
- **Delimitation** is the process of redrawing constituency boundaries based on population changes
- Done by the **Delimitation Commission** (a statutory body)
- Last full delimitation for Lok Sabha: based on 2001 Census (Delimitation Act, 2002)
- The 84th Amendment (2002) froze the total number of Lok Sabha seats at 543 until after the first Census post-2026
- Delimitation also ensures roughly equal population across constituencies within a state

## Reservation of Seats
- **Scheduled Castes (SC)**: Seats reserved proportional to SC population in each state
- **Scheduled Tribes (ST)**: Seats reserved proportional to ST population, mainly in tribal areas
- **Current reservation**: 84 seats for SC and 47 seats for ST in Lok Sabha (as of 2024)
- Reservation does not mean separate electorates — all voters in the constituency vote, but only SC/ST candidates can contest
- Reserved constituencies are rotated periodically after delimitation

## Political Parties and Symbols
### Recognition Criteria
- **National Party**: Must secure 6% of valid votes in at least 4 states AND win at least 4 Lok Sabha seats, OR win 2% of total Lok Sabha seats from at least 3 states
- **State Party**: Must secure 6% of valid votes in a state AND win 2 seats in the State Assembly, OR win 3% of total seats in the State Assembly or 3 Lok Sabha seats from the state
- **Registered Unrecognized Party**: Registered with ECI but does not meet national/state party criteria

### Symbols
- National and State parties get **reserved symbols** (exclusive use across India or the state)
- Unrecognized parties and independents are allotted **free symbols** from ECI's list
- Symbol disputes between party factions are resolved by ECI

## NOTA (None of the Above)
- Introduced in 2013 following the Supreme Court's judgment in **PUCL v. Union of India**
- Voters can press the NOTA button on the EVM to reject all candidates
- NOTA votes are counted but even if NOTA gets the highest votes, the candidate with the most votes among contestants wins
- Serves as a tool for voters to express dissent

## Electoral Bonds (Discontinued)
- Introduced in 2018 as a financial instrument for political donations
- Struck down by the Supreme Court in February 2024 as unconstitutional (violating right to information)
- Political funding now subject to ongoing reform discussions

## Anti-Defection Law (10th Schedule)
- Added by the **52nd Amendment Act, 1985**
- A member is disqualified if they voluntarily give up party membership or vote against party whip
- Exception: If at least two-thirds of the party's legislators merge with another party
- Decision on disqualification is made by the Speaker/Chairman of the House
- Aimed at preventing "horse-trading" and floor-crossing

## Election Expenditure Limits
- Set by ECI and revised periodically
- **Lok Sabha**: ₹95 lakh (for most states) to ₹75 lakh (for smaller states) as of 2024
- **Vidhan Sabha**: ₹40 lakh (for most states) to ₹28 lakh (for smaller states)
- Candidates must file expenditure accounts within 30 days of results
- Exceeding the limit can lead to disqualification
